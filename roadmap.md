# Deep research assessment of the OPD design space

## Bottom line

The strongest version of the post's thesis is well supported as a **literature prior**: on-policy data can be load-bearing because it trains on student-visited states rather than fixed teacher traces; plain OPD is often far more compute-efficient than sparse-reward RL; but vanilla token-level reverse-KL also has clear failure modes, especially when teacher supervision is locally uninformative, overly stylistic, or concentrated on a few brittle tokens rather than on outcome-relevant structure. The local results now force a narrower claim: in the 1B student / 7B teacher `gsm_symbolic` setting, the §8.1 L2 control directly challenges the simple on-policy story — off-policy SFT on teacher traces beats on-policy pure-OPD by roughly 55-72x at pass@1, even without a correctness filter. The newest papers still make the design space much less mysterious than it looked a year ago: OPD can be written as dense KL-constrained RL, standard token-level OPD is one point in a larger bias–variance family, and multiple groups have already started exploring the interior with reward extrapolation, entropy-aware losses, token selection, asymmetric updates, and black-box or rubric-based teachers. citeturn27view0turn9view0turn16search2turn24view2turn25view0turn24view1

My overall judgment is that your best ideas are **opd-different-teachers.md**, **per-token-kl-pivot-vs-style.md**, **meta-algorithm-alpha-lambda.md**, **per-task-hint-search-gepa.md**, **expert-rl-plus-opd.md**, and **prms-as-teachers.md**. Those are the ideas most likely to produce either a publishable mechanistic result or a practically useful new training recipe. By contrast, **student-beats-teacher-opd.md** is interesting but should be reframed around reward extrapolation and objective mismatch, not posed as a mystery of vanilla KL matching; and **hint-rewriter-distillation.md** plus **hint-writer-rl.md** feel more like second-wave derivatives of a stronger initial hint-learning or prompt-search project. citeturn16search2turn24view0turn35view0turn32view2turn28search0turn21search0turn38view0

## What the current evidence already says

The literature's clearest fact pattern is this. On-policy distillation can work because it corrects student behavior on the states the student actually visits, avoiding the train–test mismatch of fixed teacher-generated data. That idea goes back to the original GKD / OPD work and has since been reproduced in later public implementations and model reports, including entity["software","Qwen3","large language model family"] and entity["software","MiMo-V2-Flash","Mixture-of-Experts language model"], which use on-policy knowledge transfer as part of their post-training pipelines. Public writeups report order-of-magnitude compute savings versus comparable reasoning RL runs, even if the exact multiplier depends on whether teacher generation is counted and how teacher FLOPs are amortized. The local Exp 1 / §8.1 result is an important counterexample at this capacity gap: student-visited states can be so far from answer-shaped trajectories that the on-policy reverse-KL signal has little purchase, while fixed teacher traces remain directly imitable. citeturn27view0turn9view0turn13view0turn36view0

At the same time, the latest mechanistic work says teacher quality alone is not the right variable. The most informative recent OPD paper argues that success depends on two conditions: the teacher and student need compatible thinking patterns, and the teacher must provide genuinely new capability rather than merely being “bigger” or “higher scoring.” In their experiments, some same-family teachers were effectively distributionally indistinguishable from the student, meaning they added little useful signal despite looking stronger on headline metrics. That pushes the debate away from “SFT teacher versus RL teacher” as a binary and toward a more local question: does the teacher create informative gradients on the student’s actual error states? citeturn6view1turn17search3

The second major fact is that vanilla reverse-KL OPD is not a free lunch. Recent work shows that reverse KL is mode-seeking, reduces diversity, and becomes unstable exactly where the teacher is uncertain. One paper quantified the collapse directly: an OPD student retained only 6.8% high-entropy tokens against 18.5% in the teacher and showed unstable top-k dynamics under high-entropy teacher distributions. A separate line of work showed that practical sampled-token OPD is a biased approximation to sequence-level reverse-KL, albeit with much better variance scaling in long horizons; increasing future-reward coupling improves faithfulness to the sequence objective but also raises variance and destabilizes optimization. citeturn25view0turn24view2

The third major fact is that token importance is highly non-uniform. The TIP paper argues that OPD signal concentrates in two regions: high-entropy student tokens, where the student is uncertain, and low-entropy but high-divergence tokens, where the student is confident and wrong. In parallel, OPSD reports that without per-token clipping, stylistic tokens can dominate the signal and destabilize training, motivating explicit pointwise KL clipping. Together, these results line up with the post’s “pivot token” intuition: the dangerous region is not “all reasoning tokens” and not “all style tokens,” but a small subset of positions where tiny local choices disproportionately redirect the trajectory. citeturn6view2turn22search12turn22search4

Finally, the RL-vs-SFT geometry story is no longer just a metaphor. Recent parameter-space analyses find that RL fine-tuning updates a sparse subnetwork, often changing meaningfully only about 5%–30% of weights, while SFT produces much denser changes and larger spectral disruption. Another recent analysis argues that RL’s apparent sparsity reflects an optimization bias toward low-curvature, base-policy-proximal directions, whereas SFT changes principal weights much more aggressively. In parallel, RL’s Razor shows that on-policy RL is implicitly biased toward KL-minimal task solutions, which helps explain why it forgets less than SFT. That makes your “where does OPD sit?” question an excellent one: it is now experimentally answerable, not just conceptual. citeturn20view0turn20view1turn18search2turn18search4

## Assessment of the replication ideas

**opd-different-teachers.md** is the best first paper to run. The right hypothesis is not “teacher matters” versus “on-policy data matters.” The evidence now suggests that teacher usefulness is conditional on compatibility, novelty, and whether the supervision lands on answer-shaped trajectories the student can learn from; on-policy state coverage alone is not sufficient at the observed 1B/7B gap. I would test same-base teachers that differ only in post-training recipe—pure SFT, RL-trained, rubric-trained, and maybe a black-box verbal or judge teacher—while holding student initialization, prompts, rollout budget, and evaluation constant. My prediction is that once teacher compatibility is controlled, the gap between SFT-teacher and RL-teacher will be much smaller than many people expect on easy or medium tasks, but larger on frontier tasks where RL changes local reward ordering at crucial decision points. If you want one flagship replication that can either validate or falsify the post’s core claim, this is it. citeturn6view1turn17search3turn39search0turn39search1turn38view0

**student-beats-teacher-opd.md** is good, but only if you phrase it as an objective-mismatch paper. Vanilla KL matching by itself does not naturally explain “reward-optimal student beats teacher.” The recent G-OPD / ExOPD work does: standard OPD fixes the reward and KL terms at equal weight, while reward extrapolation lets the student move past the teacher boundary by changing that ratio. Preference-based self-distillation makes a similar point from another direction by replacing pure teacher matching with a reward-regularized target. So the publishable claim is not “students mysteriously beat teachers,” but “teacher imitation is not the same as reward maximization, and surpassing the teacher requires either reward extrapolation, better references, or richer supervision.” citeturn16search2turn24view0turn24view1

**entropy-collapse-opd-vs-rl.md** looks strong and timely. Entropy-aware OPD already established diversity collapse inside reverse-KL distillation, but the direct OPD-versus-RL comparison is still underexplored. A high-value version would compare matched-compute trajectories and measure token entropy, support size, top-k stability, and downstream Pass@k. My expectation is that OPD will collapse entropy faster than KL-regularized RL at high-uncertainty decision points, especially on reasoning tasks with many valid local continuations. That would turn a plausible intuition into an empirical diagnostic. citeturn25view0turn18search2

**per-token-kl-pivot-vs-style.md** is probably the best mechanistic paper in the set. It can directly connect TIP, sampled-token OPD’s one-token brittleness, and OPSD’s need for pointwise clipping. The core deliverable should be a taxonomy of where per-token KL mass goes: high-entropy uncertainty tokens, low-entropy high-divergence corrections, formatting/style tokens, and true “pivot” tokens whose local decision predicts large downstream reward changes. If you can show that clipping is helpful precisely because it suppresses high-KL / low-relevance tokens while preserving high-leverage pivots, that would be a real result. citeturn6view2turn24view2turn22search12turn22search4

**sparse-vs-dense-updates.md** is intellectually strong and complements the post extremely well. It is less likely to generate a brand-new training method, but more likely to generate a durable explanatory figure for the field. The key experiment is overlap: compare the RL sparse subnetwork, the SFT dense update cloud, and OPD updates under different objectives. If OPD lands near the RL subnetwork when teacher feedback is outcome-aligned, that is powerful evidence that the post’s geometry story is real. If OPD instead looks much more SFT-like, that would force a rewrite of the whole intuition. Either outcome is useful. citeturn20view0turn20view1turn18search2

## Assessment of the forward-looking proposals

**meta-algorithm-alpha-lambda.md** is the highest-upside framing project. You already have most of the ingredients in the literature: G-OPD shows OPD as dense KL-constrained RL with a reward scaling factor; recent OPD theory explicitly introduces a discounted return-to-go parameter that interpolates between local token updates and sequence-level objectives; PACED adds a separate weighting over sample difficulty. A clean token-level PG family over reward scale **α**, future coupling **λ**, and reference policy **π_T** would not just organize the space—it would turn the “unexplored interior” into an actual benchmarked map. If executed well, this is the most field-shaping idea on your list. citeturn16search2turn24view2turn26search3

**per-task-hint-search-gepa.md** is the best near-term intervention idea. The strongest evidence here is not from OPD directly but from GEPA and P2O: language-based reflection and prompt evolution can recover useful signal on hard, otherwise zero-gradient cases, and P2O shows that prompt-induced gains can be distilled back into the policy rather than left as inference-time hacks. Your Lagrangian objective, expected reward improvement minus a KL cost, is exactly the right abstraction for “good hints”: enough intervention to create learning signal, not so much that the no-hint policy learns nothing transferable. citeturn34view1turn35view0

**hint-rewriter-distillation.md** is attractive if you want something cheap and productizable, but I would not lead with it. It depends on first learning what makes a hint “bad” and what makes a rewrite “surgical.” The better way to use it is as a second-stage compression step after HiLL-, HINT-, or GEPA-style systems have generated a corpus of high-transfer and low-transfer hints. By itself, it is a nice model-assistant project; as a follow-on to adaptive hint learning, it becomes much more interesting. citeturn32view2turn32view0turn29search3

**co-evolving-hint-writer.md** is strong but complex. HiLL already jointly trains a hinter and a reasoner, and Self-Guide shows that policy and internal reward can co-evolve in agent settings. Your extra idea—an adaptive KL-budget controller—is exactly what this line needs, because otherwise the hinting process can become too helpful and stop teaching the no-hint policy anything durable. If you can formalize a controller around hint reliance or transferability, this could be a serious contribution. But it is an engineering-heavy project, not the first thing I would run. citeturn32view2turn29search2turn30search0

**hint-writer-rl.md** is the narrower cousin of the previous idea and should probably be treated as an ablation, not the centerpiece. A standalone hinter with reward proportional to correctness gain times one minus KL inflation is clean, but the literature is already moving toward joint optimization rather than isolated helper policies. I would keep it as a simplifying control condition inside the co-evolution paper. citeturn30search0turn32view2

**expert-rl-plus-opd.md** is one of the best practical bets. Multi-teacher distillation is already appearing in technical reports, CoPD argues that fully trained experts can be too behaviorally far from the student for clean consolidation, and newer OPD variants like AOPD and Uni-OPD explicitly aim to fix unreliable token-level guidance. Your proposal to place a teacher-KL term on top of local outcome RL, with a λ sweep or schedule, is very plausible as a stability mechanism for expert consolidation. I would rank this as a high-priority recipe paper, especially if you care about real systems rather than only theory. citeturn36view0turn28search0turn24view1turn33search1

**prms-as-teachers.md** is conceptually excellent and may be the most important route beyond same-family logit teachers. Process supervision gives dense step-level feedback and outperformed outcome supervision in the original PRM work. More recent rubric-based, judge-based, and verbal-distillation papers all point the same way: the “teacher” does not need to be a logit distribution. It can be a step verifier, a rubric-induced weighted pass rate, a judge score, or discrete verbal feedback. Reframing PRMs as teachers therefore looks less like a speculative metaphor and more like the natural generalization of OPD into teacher interfaces that care about correctness, not merely stylistic likelihood. citeturn21search0turn38view0turn39search1turn39search0

## The research program I would actually run

I would start with a **controlled mechanistic triad**. First, run **opd-different-teachers.md** with one student family and four teacher interfaces: same-family SFT teacher, same-family RL teacher, black-box verbal/judge teacher, and rubric/PRM teacher. Second, run **per-token-kl-pivot-vs-style.md** on the same trajectories, so that every failure case is localized to token categories rather than only aggregate accuracy. Third, run **sparse-vs-dense-updates.md** on the checkpoints from exactly those runs, so the mechanistic story covers teacher choice, token choice, and parameter geometry in one setup. That triad would tell you whether the post’s central story is true in practice. citeturn6view1turn24view2turn20view0turn39search0turn38view0

If that triad works, the next paper should be the **meta-algorithm sweep**. Treat OPD as one corner in a space with reward scaling **α**, future coupling **λ**, and reference policy choice **π_T**. Sweep the corners first: SFT-like mode-covering, OPD-like dense local matching, and RL-like sparse outcome optimization. Then probe the interior with ExOPD-style α greater than 1, discounted λ less than 1, and teacher-base versus student-base references. The point is not only to find a better recipe. It is to find which axes really matter and which ones are redundant. citeturn16search2turn24view2turn26search3

For the intervention track, I would choose **per-task-hint-search-gepa.md** before the learned hint-writer projects. GEPA and P2O already show that language-level search can recover signal from hard samples far more efficiently than pure RL. That makes prompt- or hint-search the cheapest way to test whether “privileged guidance under a KL budget” works. Only after that would I move to **co-evolving-hint-writer.md**, because then you have a target behavior and a transfer metric to train toward. citeturn34view1turn35view0turn32view2

The last basin to explore would be **PRMs as teachers** plus **expert RL plus OPD**. The first attacks the teacher-interface problem; the second attacks the expert-consolidation problem. In practice they may converge: a process or rubric teacher is exactly the kind of cheap, outcome-aligned dense supervisor that can stabilize expert RL without requiring same-tokenizer white-box logits everywhere. citeturn21search0turn38view0turn36view0turn33search1

## Experimental verdicts (2026-06-25)

The harness has now executed the tier-1 and tier-2 priorities below. This section maps each idea to its result so the ranking that follows reflects what actually happened, not just the literature prior. Headline numbers are 1B-SFT student / 7B-teacher on `gsm_symbolic` unless noted; full writeup in `RESULTS.md`.

**Tier 1 — what happened.**

- **opd-different-teachers.md (#1) — DONE, and the post's central claim is reversed at this gap.** Same-base SFT/DPO/Instruct 7B teachers all give pure-OPD p@1 ≈ 0.01 (§4); teacher *novelty/recipe is not the variable*. The §8.1 L2/L3 controls then isolated the binding variable: neither teacher novelty, nor on-policy state coverage (off-policy reverse-KD on teacher states reaches 0.298 vs on-policy OPD 0.008, a ~38× gap holding teacher + KL direction fixed), nor correctness filtering (only +0.09), nor KL direction is the driver. What separates winners from losers is whether the per-token signal lands on **coherent, answer-shaped, reachable trajectories** at all. The warm-start/sign-flip test (§8.1 follow-up 2) falsifies the obvious rescue: a competent init (p@1 0.383, on-policy rollouts 50% correct at step 1) is *also* collapsed to 0.003 by on-policy pure-OPD — the failure is a **dynamical instability of the on-policy reverse-KL signal**, init-independent, not an initial-state-coherence effect.

- **per-token-kl-pivot-vs-style.md (#4) — DONE, and the proposal's "pivot vs style" framing is wrong at the bucket level.** §5 taxonomy on 5 canonical arms: pure OPD puts **67% of |KL| mass on uncertain (high-entropy) tokens, only 17% on content**; GRPO inverts this (60% content, 13% uncertain); the clipped λ=0.10 winner sits between (45% uncertain, 32% content). The clip selectively disarms the uncertain bucket (54-79% of clip-removed mass is uncertain). There is no separate "pivot" bucket — the wrong_confident bucket is only 0.1-2% of tokens. The mechanism is real but the proposal's "find the pivot tokens" framing does not match the data; the actionable lever is **bound the heavy tail of per-token KL pushes** (§8.2 quantifies this: unclipped p99 climbs to 2-3 over training, clipped p99 sits at the cap), not "find pivots".

- **meta-algorithm-alpha-lambda.md (#6) — DONE, the interior is mapped, and it is *crossover-dominant* not strictly dominant.** The (α=1, λ=0.10, clip=1.0) point is the best in-distribution recipe (4-seed p@1 0.693, p@16 0.801; narrowly beats multi-seed GRPO 0.687/0.786) and **crosses over** off-task: on `simple_equations` GRPO wins p@1–p@16 by ~2× but the clipped λ=0.10 arm beats GRPO at p@32/p@64 at T=0.6 (§7.8). The mechanism is now quantitative (§8.2): unclipped low-λ drives kl_signal/p99 to 2-3 and collapses around step 100-150; `per_token_kl_clip=1.0` bounds the tail, the collapse recovers, and the 4-seed bimodality becomes a tight unimodal band. λ≥0.50 stays dead even clipped. Pure OPD (λ=1) is structurally dead at this gap regardless of clipping.

**Tier 2 — what happened.**

- **expert-rl-plus-opd.md (#11) — DONE as the (α=1, λ∈(0,1), clip) interior.** The headline finding is the **clip is a structural stabilizer, not an ad-hoc instrument**: unclipped low-λ is bimodal and seed-fragile (§7.2/§7.6); clipped low-λ is a tight unimodal band that matches GRPO. The expert-RL+OPD blend works *only* with the clip and *only* in a bounded low-λ band (λ∈[0.05, 0.20]); outside that band it is dead. The §7.11 PRM-reweight test (variant c) then **falsified** the conjecture that a principled per-token importance reweight could *replace* the clip: no-clip+reweight collapses harder (0.006 p@1, grad norms 3-6× higher) because a mass-preserving reweight *concentrates* the tail the clip exists to *bound*. Stacked on the clip it slightly hurts (0.157 vs 0.204). The clip is load-bearing.

- **prms-as-teachers.md (#13) — DONE (variant a, answer-conditioned OPSD; variant c, self-referential PRM-reweight).** Variant a (§7.10): answer-conditioning the teacher rescues pure OPD from the dead corner (0.029 → 0.252 p@1, 4-seed; mid-λ 0.099 → 0.269) by shifting the *teacher's* distribution onto student-reachable answer-shaped states — and shifts the optimal λ leftward (λ=0.50 stays alive where the logit teacher's λ=0.50 was dead). The teacher interface and the (α, λ) blend **interact**: picking π_T changes the right λ. But the §5 taxonomy shows OPSD-trained students have *more* uncertain-bucket mass (81-82%) not less — the rescue is *not* "shift mass to content" but "change what the uncertain-bucket signal means." Variant c (§7.11) is the negative result above. Variant (b) — a *separately-trained* step-level PRM scoring process correctness — remains untested; §7.11's structural argument predicts it too would need a bound to be safe.

- **per-task-hint-search-gepa.md (#9) — DONE (Exp 8), and it is a dead end at λ=1.** Stage 1 GEPA-style search found a winner hint ("Ensure each step logically follows from the previous, showing all calculations clearly; aim for a simple, straightforward solution." acc=0.547, kl_p99=4.881, Lagrangian score=−0.429). Stage 2 trained pure-OPD λ=1 with that hint as a fixed `condition_on` string, against a placebo (task-irrelevant tone hint) control, 3 seeds each. **3-seed matched control (jobs 126571-573, completed 2026-06-24):** best arm mean p@1 0.206 (s42 0.245, s43 0.126, s44 0.247), placebo mean 0.192 (s42 0.210, s43 0.219, s44 0.146). The placebo lands *above* the best arm's mean — if it holds, the rescue is **per-problem privilege** (§7.10's mechanism), not distribution-shift onto answer-shaped paths. Task-level hint search at λ=1 is a dead end; the §7.10 rescue does not survive removing the per-problem answer conditioning.

- **entropy-collapse-opd-vs-rl.md (#3) — DONE (§7.4/§7.8/§8.2).** The prediction "OPD is more mode-seeking than RL" is *wrong* for the clipped regime: clipping prevents the trainer from going fully reverse-KL, so the clipped low-λ arm keeps entropy (~8× GRPO's) while GRPO collapses distinct-4 to 0.02. The high token entropy is real and durable off-task, and it converts to pass@k coverage at high k (crossover at p@32/p@64 on `simple_equations` at T=0.6) but not to low-k accuracy. §7.12 (Exp 7) then shows the precision↔coverage trade **dissolves at scale**: at 7B←7B and 7B←13B the low-λ arm is simultaneously the highest-entropy and highest-p@1 arm.

- **sparse-vs-dense-updates.md (#5) — DONE (§6/§6.1-6.5/§8.3).** The strong post-thesis claim ("OPD inherits update geometry from the teacher recipe") is **falsified**: every α=1 trainer produces an RL-shaped sparse update regardless of teacher dose. The real variation is *which subspace* the update lives in, not whether the update is sparse vs dense. Three regimes: SFT-corner is the *sparsest* (top-1% mass 0.729, only 1.6% of weights moved >1e-4) and a *more extreme* sharper-tier shape; GRPO/pure-OPD are sharper-tier (concentrated essential subnetwork, graceful prune); clipped low-λ + RL baseline are broader-tier (dispersed essential subnetwork, cliff at p∈[80%, 90%]). Pure OPD is "sharper-shaped and rank-additive, but mistargeted" — pruning it *improves* p@1 5×. The geometry tracks *loss structure* (how many forces the trainer balances), not the teacher's identity.

**Tier 3 — what happened.**

- **student-beats-teacher-opd.md — DONE, reframed as reward-extrapolation/teacher-imitation gap.** Pure OPD does not beat the teacher at this gap (0.01 vs teacher 0.46); the only way the student exceeds the teacher is via the outcome branch (GRPO 0.69) or answer-conditioning (OPSD 0.25). The proposal's "surpassing the teacher" framing is not the operative mechanism; the operative mechanism is whether the per-token signal lands on reachable answer-shaped states (§8.1).

- **hint-rewriter-distillation.md / hint-writer-rl.md / co-evolving-hint-writer.md — partially obviated.** Exp 8's negative result (task-level hint search is a dead end at λ=1) removes the empirical basis for a standalone hint-writer project at this gap. The §7.10 answer-conditioning rescue shows the *privileged-info* teacher interface is what carries signal, not a learned hint; a hint-rewriter/co-evolution project would need to (a) operate at λ<1 with an outcome anchor (where Exp 4's recipe works) or (b) produce per-problem hints, not task-level ones — and (b) is what §7.10 already does with the ground-truth answer. The learned-hint frontier is now narrower than the proposal stage suggested.

**Tier 1 that is now closed by the scale test (Exp 7/7c).** The roadmap's "scale" caveat — "all results are 1B-student/7B-teacher" — is now **largely resolved**. Native 7B-SFT ← 7B-Instruct (§7.12): unclipped on-policy pure-OPD still dies (p@1 0.009, delayed onset at step ~152), the clip alone rescues it (0.323, 37× same-teacher), the on↔off-policy gap shrinks 38-55× → 1.26×, and the clipped low-λ recipe tops every k while staying the most diverse arm. Exp 7c (7B ← 13B-Instruct) then resolves the scale-vs-gap-closure confound toward *scale/gap-robust instability*: re-opening the gap does not prevent the collapse (A13 still dives to the dead zone), the clip stays the stabilizer (C13 0.403), and D13 is the best arm (p@1 0.746). The unclipped on-policy reverse-KL collapse now reproduces across 1B←7B, 7B←7B, and 7B←13B.

## Revised priority ranking (post-experiments)

With tier-1 closed, the agenda shifts from "confirm the intuitions" to "attack the frontier the experiments exposed." The frontier is precisely named in §8.4: **dense + on-policy + outcome-aligned, natively** — the clipped low-λ blend approximates it by importing unbiasedness from the outcome branch, OPSD approximates it by spending unbiasedness on privileged info, and PRM-reweight (variant c) failed to reach it because reweighting concentrates the tail the clip exists to bound. What remains genuinely untested is the next tier:

1. **PRM-as-teacher variant (b) — a separately-trained step-level PRM scoring process correctness.** §7.11's structural argument (reweight concentrates, doesn't bound) predicts it too would need a bound to be safe, but a *trained* PRM's importance is qualitatively different from the self-referential answer-info-gain `g_t` — it scores *process correctness* rather than *answer-dependence*, and its mass may be bounded enough to avoid the tail-sharpening. This is the highest-value next experiment: it directly tests whether the frontier object (dense + on-policy + outcome-aligned) is reachable by replacing the logit teacher with a process teacher, rather than by replacing the clip with a reweight. **The prediction to falsify:** a trained-PRM arm with no clip collapses like variant (c); a trained-PRM arm *with* clip matches or beats the clipped logit-teacher baseline at the matched λ. If both hold, the clip is confirmed as the universal structural stabilizer and the PRM teacher interface is the next axis to sweep.

2. **Cross-task generalization of OPSD (§7.10 open caveat iv).** The answer-conditioned rescue is measured on `gsm_symbolic` only. The §7.10 mechanism ("answer-conditioning changes what the uncertain-bucket signal means, not where the mass lives") predicts the rescue should survive cross-task if the teacher's answer-conditioning is task-agnostic. If OPSD's in-dist edge disappears off-task, the privileged-info mechanism is task-specific and the rescue is narrower than it looks. Run the §7.8 cross-task protocol (`simple_equations`) on the §7.10 OPSD ckpts.

3. **The (α, λ) interior at scale.** Exp 7/7c ran the *corners* (pure-OPD, clipped-pure-OPD, off-policy reverse-KD, low-λ clip) at 7B←7B and 7B←13B. The *interior* (α=1, λ=0.10, clip=1.0) — the best 1B recipe — has not been run at 7B or 13B. The §7.12 finding that the precision↔coverage trade dissolves at scale predicts the interior's crossover-dominance off-task (§7.8) might become *strict* dominance at 7B. This is the cleanest scale test still open.

4. **Long-horizon tasks.** Every result is `gsm_symbolic` (and one `simple_equations` cross-task). The roadmap's open question — "how far local token guidance scales into long-horizon settings" — is unanswered. The (α=1, λ=0.10, clip=1.0) recipe's pass@k crossover suggests it buys coverage; long-horizon agentic tasks are where that coverage should pay off most. A single run on a multi-step agent task (e.g. a reasoning_gym multi-hop task or a code-editing task) would test whether the crossover-dominance generalizes.

5. **The clip-vs-reweight structural-stabilizer question, more generally.** §7.11 falsified variant (c) but did not test whether *any* non-clip stabilizer works. Candidates: (a) a per-token KL penalty rather than a hard clip (smooth bound instead of a cap); (b) a reference-policy refresh cadence (the §7.6 dead-zone attractor might be escapable if the reference is periodically reset to the current policy); (c) a curriculum that ramps λ up/down around the step-100-150 collapse window (§7.6 tested step_off/step_on/linear_anneal and all failed, but a *clip-ramp* was not tried). The structural-stabilizer frontier is not exhausted by variant (c)'s failure.

## Open questions and limitations

The main limitation in the current evidence base is that much of the freshest OPD literature is still in preprint, technical-report, or blog form, and many of the strongest public numbers remain concentrated in math-reasoning settings with clean verifiers. That means some of the practical conclusions are high-confidence for reasoning-style tasks but weaker for open-ended instruction following, agentic environments, or domains where correctness is only partially verifiable. The black-box, rubric, verbal, and judge-based lines are promising exactly because they may generalize better there, but they are newer and less standardized than same-family white-box OPD. citeturn33search8turn9view0turn36view0turn39search0turn39search1turn38view0

The other open question is how far local token guidance can scale into long-horizon settings. The recent OPD theory papers argue that token-level objectives win on variance but lose information about future return, and several of the new methods can be read as attempts to patch that gap without paying full sequence-level variance costs. Your α–λ meta-algorithm is valuable precisely because it would test whether that tradeoff is fundamental or only an artifact of today’s estimators. If I had to summarize the whole landscape in one sentence, it would be this: the most promising frontier is no longer “OPD versus RL,” but **how to get dense, on-policy, outcome-aligned supervision without collapsing to brittle local imitation**.  

## Post-experiment open questions (2026-06-25)

The literature-assessment questions above have now been tested in the harness. What the experiments *closed* and what remains *open* has shifted, so this section records the post-experiment frontier separately from the literature prior above.

**Closed by the harness (Exp 1-8, §4-§8.4):**

- **Teacher recipe is not the variable at this gap.** Same-base SFT/DPO/Instruct teachers all give pure-OPD p@1 ≈ 0.01 (§4); teacher novelty is off the critical path. What separates winners from losers is whether the per-token signal lands on coherent, answer-shaped, reachable trajectories (§8.1 L2/L3).
- **On-policy state coverage is not load-bearing at the 1B/7B gap — the post's headline reverses.** Off-policy reverse-KD on teacher states reaches 0.298 vs on-policy OPD 0.008 (~38×), holding teacher + KL direction fixed (§8.1 L3). The warm-start test falsifies the competence rescue (0.383 → 0.003). The failure is a dynamical instability of the on-policy reverse-KL signal, init-independent.
- **The clip is a structural stabilizer, not an ad-hoc instrument.** `per_token_kl_clip=1.0` bounds the heavy tail of per-token KL pushes (§8.2: unclipped p99 climbs to 2-3, clipped p99 sits at the cap); without it the low-λ interior is bimodal and seed-fragile (§7.6), with it the 4-seed band is tight and matches GRPO (§7.7). The PRM-reweight attempt to *replace* the clip with a principled importance reweight failed structurally (§7.11: reweight concentrates the tail the clip exists to bound).
- **The (α=1, λ=0.10, clip=1.0) interior is crossover-dominant, not strictly dominant.** In-distribution it narrowly beats GRPO (0.693 vs 0.687); off-task GRPO wins p@1-p@16 by ~2× but the interior crosses over at p@32/p@64 (§7.8). The precision↔coverage trade dissolves at scale (§7.12: at 7B←7B and 7B←13B the low-λ arm is simultaneously highest-entropy and highest-p@1).
- **Update geometry tracks loss structure, not teacher recipe.** The strong post-thesis claim ("OPD inherits update geometry from the teacher") is falsified (§6/§8.3): every α=1 trainer is RL-sparse regardless of teacher dose; SFT-from-rollouts is the *sparsest* of all. Three regimes (SFT-corner / sharper / broader) differ in which subspace the update lives in, not whether the update is sparse.
- **Scale closes the on↔off-policy gap but not the collapse.** The unclipped on-policy reverse-KL collapse is scale-robust (1B←7B, 7B←7B, 7B←13B all die at p@1 ≈ 0.01); the clip rescues it at every scale; the on↔off gap shrinks 38-55× → 1.26× at 7B←7B (§7.12). "On-policy is a liability" is a small-student/large-gap artifact, conditional on the clip.
- **Task-level hint search is a dead end at λ=1.** Exp 8's 3-seed matched control (jobs 126571-573) shows the searched winner hint (0.206) does not beat the placebo tone hint (0.192); the §7.10 rescue is per-problem privilege, not distribution-shift.

**Now open (post-experiments):**

1. **PRM-as-teacher variant (b) — a separately-trained step-level PRM.** The self-referential answer-info-gain `g_t` (variant c) failed because reweighting concentrates the tail. A trained PRM scoring *process correctness* rather than *answer-dependence* is qualitatively different and may have bounded mass. **Prediction to falsify:** no-clip+trained-PRM collapses like variant (c); trained-PRM+clip matches or beats the clipped logit-teacher baseline at matched λ. This is the highest-value next experiment — it directly tests whether the frontier object (dense + on-policy + outcome-aligned) is reachable by swapping the teacher interface rather than swapping the stabilizer.

2. **Cross-task generalization of OPSD.** The §7.10 answer-conditioned rescue is measured on `gsm_symbolic` only. Run the §7.8 cross-task protocol (`simple_equations`) on the §7.10 OPSD ckpts. If the rescue survives cross-task, the privileged-info mechanism is task-agnostic; if not, it is task-specific and narrower.

3. **The (α, λ) interior at scale.** Exp 7/7c ran the *corners* at 7B←7B and 7B←13B. The *interior* (α=1, λ=0.10, clip=1.0) — the best 1B recipe — has not been run at 7B or 13B. §7.12's finding that the precision↔coverage trade dissolves at scale predicts the interior's crossover-dominance might become *strict* dominance at 7B. 4. **Long-horizon tasks.** Every result is `gsm_symbolic` (and one `simple_equations` cross-task). A single run on a multi-step agent task would test whether crossover-dominance generalizes.

The structural-stabilizer frontier is not exhausted by variant (c)'s failure.

6. **The black-box / rubric / judge teacher interface.** The literature prior above flagged black-box, rubric, and judge-based teachers as promising for domains where correctness is only partially verifiable.
